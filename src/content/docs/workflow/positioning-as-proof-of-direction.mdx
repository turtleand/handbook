---
title: Positioning as Proof of Direction
description: Make serious work easier to understand, remember, and trust by leaving a visible trail of useful artifacts.
---

Positioning is not fake branding. It is the accumulated proof that helps people understand what the work is really about.

Do not claim a position first and hunt for evidence later. Build useful artifacts until the direction becomes visible.

## The mental model

Think of positioning as a trail of proof.

Every article, lesson, repo, diagram, note, map, or principle should make the path easier to see. It should show what you are learning, building, noticing, refusing, and becoming less generic about.

## What counts as proof

Useful proof can be small:

1. **Essays** that clarify judgment.
2. **Lessons** that teach reusable ideas.
3. **Technical notes** that show craft.
4. **Repos and demos** that verify capability.
5. **Maps and principles** that compress taste.

The point is not to look busy. The point is to make direction legible through substance.

## What to avoid

Avoid vague self-description unsupported by artifacts.

Avoid performative branding that says more than the work can prove.

Avoid exposing private strategy, private systems, or sensitive operating detail just to seem transparent.

Avoid treating discoverability as a guarantee. Public proof improves the odds that the right people can understand the work, but it does not force attention.

## Operating rule

When the work feels hard to explain, do not start with a bigger slogan. Create a clearer artifact.

The right artifact should answer one question for the next serious reader: what does this work prove about the direction?

## Bottom line

Good positioning helps the right people see the path without needing the full backstory.

The artifact is the signal. The direction compounds when the signal repeats.
